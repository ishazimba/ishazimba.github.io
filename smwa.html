STUDENT MENTAL WELLBEING APPLICATION (SMWA)

Problem Statement

This project focuses on the design and development of a Student Mental Well-Being Application, with the aim of addressing the critical issue of mental well-being among university students. Extensive research has shown that mental well-being plays a significant role in an individual's emotional, psychological, and social state, influencing their thoughts, feelings, and reactions to various situations. Given the high prevalence of mental well-being disorders among university students, with estimates ranging from 12% to 46% affected annually (Harrer et al., 2019), this project seeks to understand the specific needs, challenges, experiences, and behaviors of these students.

User Research

In order to gain a deeper understanding of the user and inform the development process, a comprehensive approach was undertaken, utilizing an online survey specifically targeted at university students. The research findings were meticulously evaluated and served as a foundation for developing essential artifacts, including the empathy map and storyboard. These artifacts, combined with the insights derived from the research, played a pivotal role in identifying both the functional and non-functional requirements of the Student Mental Well-being Application (SMWA).

The primary aim was to create a high-fidelity prototype of an SMWA that effectively addresses the daily needs and aspirations of its users, empowering them to manage their mental well-being more effectively. The project adopted a professional and systematic approach to contribute to the well-being and success of university students by providing them with a user-centric and purposeful digital solution.

User Research Findings

The UX research survey revealed significant findings regarding the impact on students' mental well-being. International students faced challenges adapting to a new environment, study system, and culture, resulting in negative well-being. Communication difficulties, isolation, and anxiety from coursework and deadlines also contributed to increased stress.

Coping strategies included seeking support from friends and family, engaging in personal activities, and desiring a confidential forum for connecting with others facing similar issues. Respondents emphasized the importance of an application that tracks emotional well-being, offers time management tips, and provides a confidential space.

Additionally, the survey explored the relationship between colors and emotions. Respondents overwhelmingly identified blue as having the most positive impact, followed by white and green. These findings inform the design of the Student Mental Well-being Application, with blue being the chosen base color to evoke a positive user experience. Overall, the research provides valuable insights for developing a user-centric and effective solution to support the emotional well-being of university students.

Empathy Map

The empathy map provides a visual representation of the users' perspectives, allowing for a deeper exploration of their goals, challenges, and motivations related to mental well-being. By synthesizing the survey data, distinct personas were formed to represent different user segments. Each persona within the empathy map encapsulates specific user characteristics and experiences, shedding light on their unique requirements. This helps guide the design process and ensures that the SMWA addresses the users' specific needs effectively. The empathy map serves as a valuable reference throughout the design journey, enabling the team to maintain a user-centered approach. It facilitates the identification of opportunities to enhance the user experience, tailor features to address their needs, and create an application that fosters a supportive environment for mental well-being. In summary, the empathy map derived from the survey results provides a concise and professional tool to understand the users' perspectives, allowing for the development of the SMWA in a manner that is aligned with their requirements and aspirations.

Story Board

The storyboard created for the SMWA (Student Mental Well-Being Application) revolves around the narrative of Ellie, a new university student who finds herself facing numerous challenges that are taking a toll on her mental well-being. Ellie's journey through her university life is portrayed, highlighting the difficulties she encounters and the impact they have on her overall state of mind.

The storyboard begins by introducing Ellie as a bright and ambitious young woman embarking on her university journey. However, as the story unfolds, it becomes evident that she is struggling with a multitude of issues. The weight of heavy workloads, the unfamiliarity of a new environment, homesickness, the pressure of assignments, difficulty in making friends in an unfamiliar place, and a recent breakup all contribute to the overwhelming nature of her university experience.

Each frame of the storyboard focuses on significant moments in Ellie's journey, capturing the emotions she experiences and the challenges she faces. The visuals portray Ellie's frustration, anxiety, and feelings of isolation as she navigates her way through these obstacles. The storyboard acts as a visual representation of the hardships Ellie encounters and provides a clear narrative that resonates with many students who have undergone similar experiences.

As Ellie's story unfolds, she reaches a turning point where she decides to confide in one of her colleagues about the struggles she's been facing. This pivotal moment marks a crucial step towards seeking help and finding a solution. Ellie's colleague suggests that she download the Student Mental Well-Being Application, recognizing its potential to improve her mental well-being.

The subsequent frames of the storyboard depict Ellie's journey as she discovers the SMWA and begins using it to address her specific needs and concerns. The purpose of the storyboard is to communicate the relevance and significance of the SMWA in addressing the unique challenges faced by students like Ellie. As a UX designer, the storyboard is crafted in a professional and engaging manner, employing clear and smooth visual storytelling techniques. The visuals and accompanying narrative aim to evoke empathy, understanding, and a sense of connection with the target audience, highlighting the potential of the SMWA to provide meaningful support throughout their university journey.

By presenting Ellie's story in a coherent and relatable manner, the storyboard effectively communicates the benefits and value of the SMWA as a trusted companion for students, promoting their mental well-being and helping them thrive in their academic and personal lives.

Prototype Link

The application was intended to be used by students seeking mental well-being tips, suggestions, and solutions to cope with their situation. The user can check in their status once they enter into the homepage application. The homepage of the application has the following navigation bars:

  • Peace of mind: Through this navigation bar, the user can get access to meditation, soothing music, breathing exercises, music for concentration, sound of nature, and binaural beats.
  • Want to talk: This allows the user to either chat with a professional counselor or join the discussion forum where they can share their issues anonymously.
  • Tips: This navigation bar takes the user to a page where they can find different tips and techniques to improve their well-being.
  • Sound sleep: With this navigation bar, the user can get access to sleep podcasts, bedtime stories, and music that helps them fall asleep.
  • Your Reports: The user can view their weekly report on stress level, anxiety level, sleep pattern, and focus level.
  • Get Info: The user can find information about mental well-being, NHS services, UWE well-being services, and JobShop student union.

With the hamburger icon, the user can change settings, view their profile page, and sign out from the application.

To evaluate the design and user-friendliness of the application, a usability test was performed among university students. The prototype evaluation sheets for each version of the prototype were based on the test task scenario provided to the participants during usability testing. The Concurrent Think Aloud (CTA) technique was used to understand the participants' thoughts while interacting with the application prototype. Participants were instructed to think and talk out loud, expressing their thoughts and walking through their processes. For buttons and navigation bars without interactivity, participants were encouraged to express their expectations of what they would see when interacting with them.
